Setting Your Pricing Strategy

üí° Goal: Be competitive while ensuring you cover costs and maintain healthy profit margins.

Key Pricing Factors

üìç Location & Property Type

High-value areas and larger multi-unit buildings can command higher fees.

üíµ Rent Amount

Typically a percentage of monthly rent, so higher rent = higher fee.

üéØ Service Level

More comprehensive packages justify higher percentage fees.

üìä Portfolio Size

Larger portfolios may get discounts due to economies of scale.

üîß Property Condition

High-maintenance or older properties may warrant premium pricing.

‚ûï Additional Services

Leasing fees, evictions, and STR services add to base pricing.

Typical Pricing Models

% Percentage of Monthly Rent

Range: 8-15% of monthly rent depending on services

When to use: Most common for ongoing management. Scales with property value.

üè† Leasing/Placement Fee

Range: 50-100% of one month's rent

When to use: One-time fee when placing a new tenant. Compensates for marketing, showings, screening.

üí≤ Flat Monthly Fee

Range: Fixed amount per property (e.g., $150-$300)

When to use: Low-rent units or fixed-scope services where percentage doesn't make sense.

+ Markup on Vendor Invoices

Range: 5-10% markup on maintenance costs

When to use: Coordination fee for managing vendors and maintenance.

üìè Minimum Fee

Range: $125-$150/month floor

When to use: Ensures you cover overhead even for very low-rent properties.

Legal & Licensing Requirements

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Legal Disclaimer: This is general information, not legal advice. Always consult your state licensing body or legal counsel for your specific situation.

üåµ Arizona

Licensing Authority: Arizona Department of Real Estate (ADRE)
License Required: Real estate broker or salesperson license needed if managing rental properties for others (collecting rent, negotiating leases, overseeing maintenance)
Broker License Requirements:
  • Age 18+
  • Complete 90 hours pre-license education
  • Pass licensing exam
  • Background/fingerprint check
Additional: For short-term lodging or commercial properties, a PMC (Property Management Company) TPT (Transaction Privilege Tax) license is required
Ongoing: Continuing education, trust-account rules, recordkeeping compliance
Key Takeaway: If managing properties for others in Arizona, you'll almost certainly need a real estate license. Self-managing your own properties is typically exempt.

‚≠ê Texas

Licensing Authority: Texas Real Estate Commission (TREC)
License Required: Real estate salesperson or broker license required if showing/leasing for others OR accepting rent deposits on behalf of owners
Salesperson License Requirements:
  • Age 18+
  • High school diploma or GED
  • Complete 180 hours pre-licensing education
  • Pass licensing exam
  • Obtain sponsorship by a broker
Company Licensing: Business entity should be licensed as a real estate broker if handling properties for others
Exemptions: If you're an employee of the owner and the owner is licensed, you may not need your own license
Key Takeaway: Full property management for other owners (leasing, rent collection, maintenance) requires a real estate license. Managing only your own properties or doing maintenance-only work may be exempt.

üèà Alabama

Licensing Authority: Alabama Real Estate Commission (AREC)
License Required: Any person or company engaged in rental leasing and/or property management services for others must hold a real estate license
Salesperson License Requirements:
  • Age 19+
  • High school diploma or GED
  • Complete 60 hours pre-licensing education
  • Pass licensing exam
Post-Licensing: Within first year, complete post-license education. Then continuing education required for renewals
Key Takeaway: Similar to TX & AZ - managing other people's properties requires a license. Managing only your own properties typically does not trigger licensing requirements (but verify with local law).
